International Governance of Artificial Intelligence

Understanding Global Legal Frameworks, Risks, and Cooperation Models

Global AI Governance: Why It Matters

  • AI is a borderless technology with global impacts
  • Domestic laws alone can't address transboundary AI risks
  • Need for international rules to reduce legal fragmentation and regulatory arbitrage
  • Examples from nuclear and digital privacy governance show international regulation is possible

Challenges of Governing AI Internationally

  • AI’s scale and opacity make harms hard to trace
  • Cross-border data and infrastructure weaken clear accountability
  • Governance power is concentrated in few states and companies
  • Uncertainty around surveillance, warfare, and misinformation uses fuels instability

Current Global Governance Landscape

  • Fragmented efforts: OECD Principles, GPAI, G7, Council of Europe
  • Diverse national approaches (EU, US, China) cause legal asymmetry
  • New instruments like the Bletchley Declaration show some convergence
  • Global institutional coherence is urgently needed

International Legal Instruments: Treaties

  • Treaties create binding obligations under international law
  • The Council of Europe AI Convention (2024) is the first binding AI treaty
  • Requires transparency, accountability, and human rights protections
  • States can make reservations, but not undermine the treaty’s purpose

Challenges in Treaty Negotiation and Implementation

  • Divergent philosophies: precautionary (EU), innovation-led (US), state-directed (China)
  • Broad scope and limited enforceability make consensus difficult
  • Overbroad reservations can weaken treaty impact
  • Many states lack technical capacity to implement treaty obligations

Soft Law and Voluntary Frameworks

  • OECD AI Principles (2019) and UNESCO Ethics Recommendation (2021) are key soft law tools
  • Soft law helps shape domestic laws and build consensus
  • Bletchley Declaration and G7 Hiroshima Process promote voluntary commitments
  • Soft law is flexible but lacks enforceability

Influence and Limits of Soft Law

  • Shapes national laws and sets international expectations
  • Can lay groundwork for future treaties
  • Builds capacity and interoperability
  • Risks include inconsistent adoption and dominance by powerful states or firms

Institutional and Hybrid Governance Models

  • GPAI supports research sharing, policy advice, and multi-stakeholder dialogue
  • AI Safety Institutes (UK, US) test and evaluate high-risk models
  • Transatlantic TTC aligns EU–US approaches to risk and transparency
  • Bilateral agreements promote joint evaluations and safety cooperation

Institutional Cooperation and Technical Alignment

  • Shared protocols, benchmarks, and red teaming help standardize model evaluation
  • Safety institutes act as proto-regulators, but lack binding authority
  • Success depends on voluntary cooperation from states and companies

Multistakeholder Governance Models

  • Bodies like GPAI include states, firms, and civil society
  • Operate without legal mandates; legitimacy depends on transparency and inclusiveness
  • Some proposals call for formal authority under future treaty mechanisms

Proposal: Global AI Regulator (GAISA)

  • Modeled after the IAEA, proposed to oversee frontier AI globally
  • Tasks would include inspections, certifications, safety monitoring, and capacity-building
  • Backed by treaty commitments and enforcement mechanisms

Feasibility and Comparison with IAEA

  • IAEA succeeds through clear mandate, treaty authority, and Security Council backing
  • GAISA would face challenges with software-based systems and sovereignty concerns
  • Could begin as a technical body and evolve into a full regulator

Timeline of Key Developments in Global AI Governance

  • 2017–2020: Early soft law (Asilomar, OECD, G20, GPAI)
  • 2021: UNESCO Ethics Recommendation adopted by 193 states
  • 2023: Bletchley Declaration, G7 Code of Conduct, AI Safety Institutes launched
  • 2024: Council of Europe AI Convention adopted, Seoul AI Summit commitments
  • 2025: Ongoing UN Digital Compact negotiations, export controls expand

Legal Doctrines and Principles in AI Governance

  • Sovereignty and extraterritoriality challenged by transboundary AI harms
  • Due diligence: states must prevent harm beyond their borders
  • Attribution: responsibility can extend to state-facilitated private actors
  • Precautionary principle supports early regulation in the face of risk

Role of Soft Law, Lex Specialis, and Legal Pluralism

  • Soft law shapes practice and may evolve into customary international law
  • Lex specialis gives precedence to AI-specific norms in legal conflicts
  • Legal pluralism reflects overlapping laws, treaties, and standards in global governance

Human Rights and AI

  • Right to privacy threatened by mass biometric and behavioral surveillance
  • Right to equality violated by biased or discriminatory algorithms
  • Right to due process undermined by opaque and unaccountable automated decisions

Extraterritorial Human Rights Obligations

  • States may be liable for AI harms abroad if they exert effective control
  • UN Guiding Principles expect states to regulate corporate human rights impacts
  • Exporting harmful AI systems can trigger state responsibility

Trade and Investment Law and AI

  • WTO rules govern cross-border AI services, IP, and standards
  • AI-related regulations may be challenged under GATS or TRIPS
  • General exceptions allow regulation for health, public morals, or safety

Export Controls and National Security

  • US and others impose export controls on AI chips and models
  • National security exceptions to trade rules are sensitive and rarely litigated
  • Dual-use debates intensify as AI tools gain strategic significance

Intellectual Property Challenges

  • AI-generated content may not qualify for copyright
  • Laws differ on whether human-guided outputs can be protected
  • AI training raises legal issues over scraping copyrighted content

Patent and Trade Secret Protections

  • Patent laws generally exclude non-human inventors
  • Trade secrets often protect model architectures and training methods
  • Tension between transparency needs and IP rights persists

International Humanitarian Law and Autonomous Weapons

  • AI-enabled weapons must respect distinction and proportionality
  • State and individual responsibility apply for IHL violations
  • Debate ongoing on banning or regulating lethal autonomous weapons

AI and Environmental Law

  • Large models require high compute, energy, and water use
  • Environmental impacts often fall on low-regulation states
  • No binding treaty yet mandates environmental reviews for AI

Data Protection and Privacy

  • GDPR regulates AI data use with extraterritorial scope
  • Automated decisions must be explainable, contestable
  • Convention 108+ offers global data protection baseline

AI-Specific Privacy Risks

  • AI can infer sensitive data from non-sensitive inputs
  • Consent, transparency, and contextual integrity are undermined
  • Regulatory enforcement is uneven across jurisdictions

Criminal and Liability Law

  • AI harms raise attribution and causation challenges
  • Civil liability (e.g., tort, negligence) more common than criminal
  • EU exploring AI-specific liability directives and presumptions

Cybersecurity and Dual-Use Issues

  • AI can enable cyber attacks: phishing, malware, disinformation
  • Attribution to developers is difficult without clear intent
  • Export controls and cyber norms aim to address dual-use risks

Competition Law and AI Market Power

  • Dominant AI firms control data, compute, and distribution
  • Practices like bundling and self-preferencing raise antitrust concerns
  • Essential facilities doctrine may apply to foundational models

Antitrust Enforcement Trends

  • UK, US, and EU examining AI’s effect on competition
  • Concerns about early mover advantages and platform lock-in
  • No binding global antitrust regime; coordination remains weak

AI in Space and Telecom Law

  • Outer Space Treaty holds states responsible for AI-enabled satellites
  • AI in orbit may pose risks in surveillance, targeting, and interference
  • Liability Convention applies to AI-caused damage in space

Dispute Resolution and Enforcement

  • ISDS allows firms to sue states for discriminatory AI regulations
  • WTO handles disputes over trade-restrictive AI measures
  • ICJ may hear state-to-state cases involving AI, with consent

Domestic Enforcement of International AI Norms

  • Soft law must be implemented via national legislation
  • National regulators enforce AI rules through audits and fines
  • Courts may use international norms to interpret domestic laws

Actors in International AI Governance

  • States and regions (e.g., EU) set standards and negotiate treaties
  • International bodies (OECD, UNESCO, ITU) coordinate and guide
  • Private firms and civil society influence policy and fill governance gaps

The Role of Lawyers: Legislative & International Design

  • Draft AI-specific legislation (licensing rules, audit mandates)
  • Advise lawmakers on constitutional, trade, and human rights implications of AI policy
  • Help design oversight systems (regulatory agencies, public procurement rules)
  • Negotiate AI treaties and digital trade chapters at international level
  • Ensure treaty language balances sovereignty with cross-border accountability
  • Advise on opt-outs, reservations, and treaty compliance with existing obligations

The Role of Lawyers: Litigation & Corporate Practice

  • Represent clients in litigation involving AI-related harms, bias, or misuses
  • Handle civil, administrative, human rights, and investor-state disputes involving AI
  • Deal with explainability, causation, and evidence challenges in black-box systems
  • Design internal AI governance programs and manage compliance in corporate settings
  • Draft terms of use, data protection strategies, and handle dual-use risks
  • Anticipate liability exposure under cybersecurity, IP, and product safety laws

The Role of Lawyers: Ethics, Education & Institutions

  • Contribute to development of soft law and technical standards (ISO, IEEE, OECD)
  • Translate ethical principles into enforceable legal practices
  • Educate future lawyers on AI law, ethics, and data governance
  • Publish scholarship and support civil society via hearings and consultations
  • Help build institutional infrastructure (AI oversight bodies, redress platforms)
  • Draft bylaws and governance rules for new AI-specific agencies and mechanisms

Global Gaps and Future Directions

  • Global South lacks voice and control in AI rule-making
  • Digital sovereignty and data justice require inclusive governance
  • Labor rights, emergency AI use, and environmental harm need attention

Future Governance Needs

  • Public-interest institutions, open infrastructure, and shared resources
  • Environmental impact standards for large models
  • Legal mechanisms for community data rights and equitable participation

Conclusion: Synthesis and Outlook

  • AI governance is evolving through treaties, soft law, and institutions
  • Fragmentation and inequality persist, requiring stronger cooperation
  • Legal pluralism, interdisciplinary literacy, and global solidarity are key
