International AI Governance Timeline

2017

Asilomar AI Principles

Published by the Future of Life Institute. While non-binding, they serve as one of the earliest multistakeholder ethical frameworks for AI.

EU High-Level Expert Group on AI formed

Begins drafting ethical guidelines and proposing policy foundations for what would become the EU AI Act.

2018
2019

Key Developments

  • OECD Council Recommendation on Artificial Intelligence adopted by 42 countries. It becomes the first intergovernmental standard-setting instrument on AI.
  • G20 adopts the OECD AI Principles, signaling early normative convergence at the highest political level.

Key Developments

  • Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) launched with the OECD as secretariat. It operationalizes the OECD Principles through working groups on responsible AI, data governance, and innovation.
  • UN Secretary-General’s Roadmap for Digital Cooperation recommends beginning discussions on global AI governance mechanisms.
2020
2021

Key Developments

  • UNESCO adopts its Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence, unanimously endorsed by 193 member states. This becomes the most widely supported international soft-law instrument on AI.
  • Council of Europe’s Ad Hoc Committee on Artificial Intelligence (CAHAI) releases its feasibility study for a binding legal instrument.

Key Developments

  • EU reaches political agreement on core components of the AI Act, the world’s first attempt at a horizontal, risk-based AI regulation with extraterritorial scope.
  • United States Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights published by the White House OSTP. While not binding, it outlines federal priorities and sectoral guidelines.
2022
2023

Key Developments

  • G7 Hiroshima AI Process launched to develop shared policy frameworks and an International Code of Conduct for Advanced AI.
  • Bletchley Declaration on AI Safety signed by 28 countries, including China and the US, during the UK-hosted AI Safety Summit. It recognizes the need for international collaboration on frontier AI risks.
  • US Department of Commerce initiates export control measures on AI chips and computing infrastructure, citing national security and proliferation risks.
  • AI Safety Institutes launched in the UK and US, formalizing technical collaboration on model evaluation and red-teaming.

Key Developments

  • Council of Europe adopts the Framework Convention on AI, Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law, the first binding multilateral AI treaty.
  • GPAI expands to 29 members and launches model evaluation task force.
  • Seoul AI Safety Summit issues further commitments on information sharing and technical evaluations.
  • UN Secretary-General proposes creation of a High-Level Advisory Body on AI, setting groundwork for institutional coordination under the UN umbrella.
2024
2025

Expected Developments

  • Ongoing negotiations for UN Global Digital Compact, which includes AI governance as a pillar, are expected to produce new institutional proposals.
  • Export control regimes, including Wassenaar Arrangement discussions, begin to formally address general-purpose AI technologies.
